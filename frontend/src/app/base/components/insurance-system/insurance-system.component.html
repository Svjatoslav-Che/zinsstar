Are deposits at risk?
Deposit insurance is an important pillar for stable financial markets. It is a legally anchored commitment to the EU-wide protection of savers and to maintaining stability within the European Union. The basis for this is provided by the EU directives 2009/14 / EC and 2014/49 / EU), which all member states must implement. This security scheme protects savings deposits of up to EUR 100,000 per customer and per bank. This deposit protection is also generally guaranteed for overnight money and fixed-term deposits.

Deposit protection applies throughout the EU
The statutory deposit insurance for fixed-term deposits and other bank deposits is regulated within the European Union within the framework of EU directives. They provide for certain minimum requirements for depositor protection, which were implemented in each case by national laws. In Germany this is the Deposit Protection and Investor Compensation Act. Other EU countries have their own implementation regulations.

What is deposit insurance?
The statutory deposit insurance within the European Union is an expression of the political will to protect European savers from the consequences of a possible bankruptcy. This means that bank deposits are secured up to the equivalent of EUR 100,000 per customer and bank . This means that if a bank becomes insolvent and can no longer repay deposits, savers receive their money back in full up to the protection limit via the statutory deposit guarantee system. This compensation scheme primarily serves to protect private investors and companies.

In addition to the minimum requirements of the statutory deposit protection, many credit institutions offer additional voluntary deposit protection by paying into additional deposit protection funds. In addition, the state guarantee plays an important role in security.

All over Europe, the minimum requirements for the legally guaranteed deposit insurance have been further developed for years in order to improve the protection of investors in the European Union: Since July 2015, they have been implemented successively in all EU member states.

Deposit insurance in non-EU countries
Banks from non-EU countries can be set up completely differently when it comes to deposit insurance. First, as an investor, you have to deal with when a bank comes under European law. For example, while branches of German or EU banks are subject to the statutory deposit insurance of the EU despite their location in a non-EU country, this does not count for subsidiaries. In addition to the legal regulations, the creditworthiness of the countries must also be observed.
Deposit insurance in Germany
Due to the comparatively complicated banking system in Germany, the statutory deposit insurance is also much more complex in this country than in other EU countries. In addition to public and private banks, there are also savings banks and cooperative banks in Germany. Each of these banking associations has its own deposit guarantee system.

Public banks
Members of the Federal Association of Public Banks in Germany (VÖB) rely on their own deposit insurance. The compensation scheme of the Federal Association of Public Banks in Germany (EdÖ) is responsible for this . Apart from this, numerous public banks are members of the voluntary deposit protection fund.
Savings banks and cooperative banks
Savings banks and cooperative banks do not rely on the statutory deposit insurance. Instead, the institute protection applies to this banking association . If a bank in this group gets into financial difficulties, the remaining members step in. The institute security is intended to prevent a compensation event from occurring in the first place
Deposit insurance in Germany
Banks that have a branch in Germany but are headquartered abroad are not subject to German deposit insurance. However, the foreign banks have the option of voluntarily joining the deposit protection fund of the Association of German Banks and providing additional protection for bank deposits.
Compensation in other EU countries was significantly simplified in 2015 . In the event of bank insolvency, investors no longer have to deal with the compensation scheme in the respective country. The compensation runs automatically through the German deposit insurance system.
In an international comparison, deposit insurance in Germany and other EU countries is considered very secure.
In addition, by 2024 the statutory payout period through deposit protection funds must be reduced from the current 20 to 7 working days . This has already been implemented in some countries. Overall, however, the process can take a few weeks longer, as it must first be determined whether there is actually a case for the deposit insurance.
Processing and payouts by the deposit insurance
In the event of damage, the respective country's deposit insurance is obliged to make the payment within a maximum of 21 days - this period will be gradually reduced to 7 days by 2024. This has already been implemented in some countries. Overall, however, the process can take a few weeks longer, as it must first be determined whether there is actually a case for deposit insurance.

Even more security to protect depositors in the future
As part of the initiative towards a banking union, the ECB has taken on European banking supervision to ensure that state intervention to rescue the banking system will be less necessary in the future. By 2024 at the latest, the available financial resources of the respective deposit protection fund must correspond to at least 0.8% of the deposits covered .
